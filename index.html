Section 01

Background & Biography

Harriet Tubman was born in March 1822 in Dorchester County, Maryland, originally named Araminta Ross. She was one of eleven children born to enslaved parents, Harriet and Benjamin Ross.

From age five, she was employed by her master — first as a nursemaid, then as a field hand, cook, and woodcutter. At around age twelve, an overseer threw an iron weight at another enslaved worker, striking Tubman instead and nearly crushing her skull. The injury caused seizures and sudden sleep episodes for the rest of her life, which she interpreted as spiritual experiences.

In 1844, she married a free Black man known as John Tubman and took his last name. In 1849, fearing that she and others on the plantation would be sold, she fled north — but her husband refused to join her. She followed the North Star to Philadelphia, found work, and saved money to return and free others.

After the Civil War, she permanently settled in Auburn, New York, where she had also brought her parents out of Maryland in 1857. She died of pneumonia on March 10, 1913, and was given a military funeral.

Section 02

Historical Context

Tubman's advocacy took place during one of the most unsettling periods in American history — the era of slavery. From 1831 to 1861, tens of thousands of enslaved people escaped through a network of safe houses known as the Underground Railroad, a term from the public's fascination with railroads at the time.

The network connected to the 1780s through Quaker activism, but its activity increased after the 1830s as the abolitionist movement grew. It was not a formal organization, but the routes were supported by conductors who helped others escape.

The Fugitive Slave Law of 1850

A major turning point — this law required escaped enslaved people to be returned to their masters, even from free states. Anyone who helped them risked fines or imprisonment. This forced conductors like Tubman to guide freedom seekers all the way to Canada.

Section 03

Obstacles Faced

$40,000 Bounty

By the 1850s, Maryland slaveholders offered a $40,000 reward for her capture — a massive fortune at the time.

Fugitive Slave Act

Made it illegal to help escaped slaves anywhere in the US, forcing missions to extend all the way to Canada.

Health Condition

Her constant seizures and sudden sleep episodes from the head injury made traveling dangerous and unpredictable.

Illiteracy

She could not read or write. When she heard men reading her wanted poster aloud, she pulled out a book and pretended to read to avoid being caught.

Passenger Resistance

Some people seeking freedom became frightened and wanted to turn back, which would have made the mission far more dangerous.

Racism

She faced discrimination as a Black woman even among allies when trying to provide safety for others.

Section 04

Strategies for Change

01

Underground Railroad Conducting

Tubman personally led missions into the South, guiding enslaved people north through networks of safe houses.

02

Tactical Innovation

She escaped on Saturday nights so runaway notices couldn't be printed until Monday morning, used the master's horse and buggy for the first leg, and headed south to spot potential slave catchers.

03

Armed Deterrence

Tubman carried a pistol on every mission, telling hesitant passengers they would be free or die — ensuring no one could turn back and compromise the group.

04

Public Speaking

In the late 1850s, she began speaking at abolitionist meetings in the North, using her personal stories to build awareness and cause rallies of support.

05

Published Biography

She worked with Sarah Bradford to create Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman, spreading her story to wider audiences and generating funding for her continued work.

06

Military Intelligence

During the Civil War, she gathered information on Confederate positions, leading to key raids such as the Combahee River Raid in 1863.

07

Coalition Building

She formed alliances with William Still, Frederick Douglass, John Brown, and Levi Coffin, extending her reach far beyond what she could do alone.

08

Postwar Advocacy

After the war, she lobbied for educational opportunities for freed people, fought for her military pension, and advocated for women's suffrage at the NACW convention in 1896.

Section 05

Action Plan

Specific Goal

Tubman's primary goal was to liberate as many enslaved people as possible and to accomplish the total abolition of slavery in the United States. This was driven by her pursuit of freedom, equality, and the prevention of racial segregation.

Target Audience
Three Key Groups

Enslaved people in the South — they would directly benefit from Tubman's missions.

Northern abolitionists — they could provide safe housing, funding, and political force.

The Union Army — she would later join as a spy and scout during the Civil War.

Planned Strategies

Underground Railroad conducting, public speaking at abolitionist meetings, publishing her biography to spread awareness and raise funds, and using military intelligence during the Civil War to enable major raids and liberation events.

Section 06

Take Action — What She Did

Before the Civil War, Tubman led a total of 13 missions into Maryland, guiding approximately 70 people to freedom. This included her sister and her two children, her brother, and her elderly parents. She escaped on Saturday nights, used the master's horse and wagon for the first part of the journey, and headed south to minimize conflict and spot potential slave catchers when threatened.

During the Civil War, Tubman supported the Union Army at Beaufort, South Carolina, working as a cook, nurse, laundress, scout, and spy. She built a network of formerly enslaved people to gather information on Confederate camps and supply routes.

In 1863, she led Colonel James Montgomery and approximately 150 Black soldiers on the Combahee River Raid, freeing over 700 enslaved people — the largest emancipation event of the Civil War and the first military action led by a woman in American history.

After the war, she transformed her Auburn home into the Home for Aged and Indigent Colored People, raised money for freedmen's schools, and in 1896 became a representative to the National Association of Colored Women's first convention. In 1903, she donated 25 acres of land to the African Methodist Episcopal Zion Church as a shelter for poor and homeless African Americans.

Key Alliances

William Still — Philadelphia's Underground Railroad stationmaster who documented escapes and turned them into a book.

Frederick Douglass — An enslaved man who became a leader, writer, and speaker against slavery in the US.

John Brown — White abolitionist who used direct action, including the raid on Harpers Ferry.

Levi Coffin — Quaker abolitionist who helped thousands of enslaved people through the Underground Railroad.

Section 07

Results & Long-Term Impact

2024
Awarded Brigadier General

In 2013, President Barack Obama designated portions of Maryland's Eastern Shore as the Harriet Tubman Underground Railroad National Monument — the first national monument honoring a Black woman.

On Veterans Day 2024, she was awarded the rank of Brigadier General in the Maryland National Guard, recognizing her military service during the Civil War over 100 years after her death.

Tubman was originally chosen to appear on the front of the US $20 bill, which would have made her the first African American woman on US currency — however, this did not come to pass.

Her courage and determination directly inspired the Civil Rights Movement of the 1950s and 1960s, proving that one person's moral conviction could challenge and ultimately change an entire system of oppression.

Fun Facts

Surprising and little-known details about Harriet Tubman's extraordinary life.

🚂
Never Lost a Single Passenger
In all 13 of her Underground Railroad missions, Tubman never lost a single person she was guiding to freedom.
💊
She Used Medicine to Quiet Babies
To prevent crying infants from revealing the group's location, she carried a small amount of opium to help babies fall asleep and avoid slave catchers.
🔫
She Carried a Gun on Every Mission
Tubman carried a pistol for defense on every mission. Anyone who wanted to turn back was told they would be free or die — no one could compromise the group.
🌙
Always Escaped on Saturday Night
Newspapers couldn't print runaway notices until Monday morning. This gave the group a full two-day head start before anyone could spread the news.
💰
Paid Almost Nothing for the War
After leading the Combahee River Raid, Tubman was paid so little by the Union Army that she had to sell her own baked goods to support herself.
✡️
Nicknamed "Moses" of Her People
She led enslaved people to freedom just as Moses led the Israelites out of slavery in Egypt — a comparison that stuck throughout her life.
⚔️
First Woman to Lead a US Military Operation
Tubman led the Combahee River Raid in 1863 — the largest emancipation event of the Civil War — becoming the first woman to lead a US military operation.
📜
Contributed to the 13th Amendment
Harriet supported the abolitionist cause that led to the 13th Amendment, which formally abolished slavery in the United States in 1865.
💵
Chosen for the US $20 Bill
She was originally chosen to appear on the front of the US $20 bill — making her the first African American woman on US currency — but this never happened.
