Who She Was
From Enslaved Child to Freedom Fighter

Born Araminta Ross around 1820 in Maryland, Harriet Tubman endured brutal conditions from childhood — including a near-fatal head injury at age twelve. In 1849 she escaped north alone, then dedicated her life to returning for others. She never lost a single passenger.

Why She Matters
A Legacy That Still Resonates

From the Underground Railroad to Civil War spy to women's suffrage advocate, Tubman's activism spanned decades. In 2024 she was posthumously awarded the rank of Brigadier General — over a century after her death, her story still commands recognition.

Section 01

Background & Biography

Harriet Tubman was born Araminta Ross around 1820 in Dorchester County, Maryland, one of eleven children born to enslaved parents Harriet and Benjamin Ross. From age five she was hired out by her master — first as a nursemaid, then as a field hand, cook, and woodcutter. [Web]

At around age twelve, an overseer threw an iron weight at another enslaved worker; it struck Tubman instead, nearly crushing her skull. The injury caused seizures and sudden sleep episodes for the rest of her life, which she interpreted as divine visions. [Enc]

In 1844, she married John Tubman, a free Black man, taking his last name. In 1849, fearing she and others on the plantation would be sold, she fled north — her husband refused to join her. She followed the North Star to Philadelphia, found work, and saved money to return and free others. [Web]

After the Civil War, she settled permanently in Auburn, New York, where she had placed her parents after bringing them out of Maryland in 1857. She died of pneumonia on March 10, 1913, and was given a full military funeral. [Web]

Section 02

Historical Context

Tubman's activism took place during one of the most volatile periods in American history. From roughly 1831 to 1861, tens of thousands of enslaved people escaped through a network of safe houses known as the Underground Railroad — a term adopted from the public's fascination with railroads at the time. [DB]

The Fugitive Slave Law of 1850

Required the return of escaped enslaved people to their masters even from free Northern states, and imposed fines or imprisonment on anyone who aided them. This forced conductors like Tubman to guide freedom seekers all the way to Canada. [DB]

The network had roots in Quaker activism of the 1780s but reached peak activity after the 1830s as the abolitionist movement grew. Routes were treacherous for both escapees and helpers alike. [DB]

The country was heading toward civil war, deeply divided between slave states in the South and free states in the North. Enslaved people had no legal rights and were considered property under U.S. law — making Tubman's defiance all the more extraordinary. [Ref DB]

Section 03

Obstacles Faced

$40,000 Bounty

By the mid-1850s, Maryland slaveholders offered a fortune for her capture — a sum worth over $1 million today. [Enc]

Fugitive Slave Act

Made it illegal to assist escaped slaves anywhere in the U.S., forcing missions to extend all the way to Canada. [DB]

Chronic Health Condition

Her seizures and sudden sleep episodes from the head injury made travel unpredictable and dangerous. [Web]

Illiteracy

She could not read or write — once pretending to read a book to fool men reading her wanted poster aloud. [Web]

Passenger Resistance

Some freedom seekers grew frightened and wanted to turn back, endangering the entire mission. [Enc]

Racism & Sexism

Even among allies, she faced discrimination as a Black woman operating in a deeply unequal society. [Ref DB]

Section 04

Strategies for Change

01

Underground Railroad Conducting

Personally led missions into the South, guiding freedom seekers north through a secret network of safe houses. [Book]

02

Tactical Innovation

Escaped on Saturday nights so runaway notices couldn't appear until Monday, used the master's buggy for the first leg, and carried a drug to quiet crying infants. [Web]

03

Armed Deterrence

Carried a gun and told hesitant passengers "You'll be free or die," ensuring no one could turn back and compromise the group. [Enc]

04

Public Speaking

In the late 1850s, began speaking at abolitionist meetings across the North, using her story to rally awareness and support. [Ref DB]

05

Published Autobiography

Worked with Sarah Bradford to produce two biographies (1869 & 1886), spreading her story widely and funding her ongoing work. [Ref DB]

06

Military Intelligence

Recruited formerly enslaved people to gather intelligence on Confederate positions, enabling key raids like the Combahee River Raid. [Web]

07

Coalition Building

Formed alliances with Douglass, Brown, Still, and Coffin, amplifying her reach far beyond what she could accomplish alone. [Book]

08

Postwar Lobbying & Advocacy

After the war, lobbied for freedmen's education, fought for her military pension, and advocated for women's suffrage at the NACW convention. [Web]

Section 05

Action Plan

Specific Goal

The complete liberation of as many enslaved people as possible and the total abolition of slavery. She believed this was a divine mission — she once told an interviewer: "the Lord who told me to take care of my people meant me to do it just so long as I live." [Web]

Target Audience

Enslaved people in the South — direct beneficiaries of her rescue missions. Northern abolitionists — who provided safe houses, funding, and political pressure. The Union Army — who she would later join as a spy and scout during the Civil War. [Ref DB / Enc]

Planned Strategies

Underground Railroad missions, alliance-building with abolitionist networks, public speaking to raise awareness, and using her knowledge of Southern terrain for military intelligence during the Civil War. [Book / Web]

Section 06

Take Action — What She Did

Over the decade before the Civil War, Tubman conducted approximately 13 missions into Maryland, guiding around 70 enslaved people to freedom — including her sister, her brother, and her elderly parents. She never lost a single person. [Enc]

During the Civil War, she served the Union Army at Beaufort, South Carolina from 1862, working as a cook, nurse, laundress, scout, and spy. In 1863, she guided Colonel James Montgomery and ~150 Black soldiers on the Combahee River Raid, freeing over 700 enslaved people — the first American military action led by a woman. [Web]

After the war, she transformed her Auburn home into the Home for Aged and Indigent Colored People, raised money for freedmen's schools, and in 1896 became a delegate to the National Association of Colored Women's first convention, taking up the suffragist cause. [Web]

Key Alliances

William Still (Philadelphia stationmaster) · Frederick Douglass (fellow abolitionist) · John Brown (called her "General Tubman") · Col. James Montgomery (Combahee Raid) · Sarah Bradford (biographer) · Levi Coffin (Quaker network president) [Book / Web / Enc]

Legislation & Advocacy

Supported the 13th Amendment (1865) · Fought for her military pension (granted ~30 years later) · Advocated for women's suffrage · Donated 25 acres for a shelter via the AME Zion Church [Enc / Web]

Section 07

Results & Long-Term Impact

2024
Awarded Brigadier General rank

In 2013, President Barack Obama designated portions of Maryland's Eastern Shore as the Harriet Tubman Underground Railroad National Monument — the first national monument honoring a Black woman. [Ref DB]

On Veterans Day 2024, she was posthumously awarded the rank of Brigadier General in the Maryland National Guard for her Civil War military service. [Enc]

Her image was announced for the U.S. $20 bill, making her the first African American woman to appear on the front of U.S. currency. Commemorative coins featuring her image were released in January 2024. [Ref DB]

Her legacy inspired the Civil Rights Movement of the 1950s–60s, proving that one person's moral courage could challenge an entire legal and social system. [Ref DB]

Fun Facts

Surprising, fascinating, and little-known details about Harriet Tubman's extraordinary life.

🌟0
Never Lost a Single Passenger
In all 13 of her Underground Railroad missions, Harriet Tubman never lost a single person she was guiding to freedom. She once proudly told Frederick Douglass: "I never ran my train off the track." [DB]
📖∞
She Could Not Read or Write
Tubman was illiterate her entire life, yet communicated with allies, organized missions, gathered military intelligence, and eventually gave powerful public speeches — all without being able to read a single word. [Web]
🔫1
She Carried a Gun on Every Mission
Tubman reportedly told anyone who wanted to turn back: "You'll be free or die." She was not bluffing — she carried a pistol for self-defense and to keep the group from being compromised. [Enc]
💤~12
Hit in the Head at Age 12
An overseer threw an iron weight that struck her skull. She suffered sudden, uncontrollable sleep episodes for the rest of her life — yet used this time to have what she believed were divine visions guiding her missions. [Enc]
⭐★
"General" Tubman
John Brown — the radical abolitionist — called her "General Tubman" and said she was "one of the bravest persons on this continent." In 2024, she was officially posthumously given the rank of Brigadier General. [Enc / Web]
💊🍼
She Used Medicine to Silence Babies
To prevent crying infants from revealing the group's location to slave-catchers, Tubman carried small amounts of opium to help babies sleep during the most dangerous parts of the journey. [Book]
📅Sat
Always Escaped on Saturday Nights
Because newspapers couldn't print runaway notices until Monday morning, she timed every mission to leave on Saturday nights — giving the group a full two-day head start before anyone could spread the alarm. [Web]
💰$0
She Was Paid Almost Nothing for the War
After leading the Combahee River Raid that freed 700 people, Tubman was paid so little by the Union Army that she had to sell homemade baked goods to support herself. She fought for a pension for 30 years. [Enc]
🗳️1896
Suffragist in Her Later Years
After abolition, Tubman turned to women's suffrage. In 1896, she was a delegate to the National Association of Colored Women's first annual convention, fighting for the right to vote. [Web]
