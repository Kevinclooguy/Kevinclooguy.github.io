Sections
Section 01

Background & Biography

Harriet Tubman was born on March, 1822, in Dorchester County, Maryland, originally named "Araminta Ross." She was one of eleven children born to enslaved parents, Harriet and Benjamin Ross.

From age five, she was employed by her master and was first a nursemaid. She had multiple jobs as a field hand, cook, and woodcutter. At around age twelve, while many thoughts traveled through her head, a different worker threw an iron weight at another enslaved worker. This was Tubman, nearly crushing her skull. The injury caused seizures and caused her to fall asleep suddenly occasionally, which she interpreted as spiritual experiences.

In 1844, she married a free Black man known as John Tubman and took his last name. In 1849, she feared that others, including her, on plantations would be sold. Thus, she fled north, but her husband refused to join her. She followed the North Star to Philadelphia, got a job, and saved money to return and free others.

After the Civil War, she permanently settled in New York, where she also brought her parents out of Maryland in 1857. She died of pneumonia on March 10, 1913, and was given a military funeral.

Section 02

Historical Context

Tubman's advocacy took place during one of the most unsettling periods in American history, the era of slavery. From 1831 to 1861, tens of thousands of enslaved people escaped through a network of safe houses that became known as the "Underground Railroad," which was a term from the public's fascination with railroads at the time.

The network connected to the 1780s through Quaker activism, but its activity increased after the 1830s as the abolitionist movement grew. It was not much of an organization, but the routes were full of conductors to help others and to escape.

The Fugitive Slave Law of 1850

A turning point was during the Fugitive Slave Law of 1850, which required escaped enslaved people to return to their masters, even from free states. There were many risks, such as fines or imprisonment, for anyone who helped them. This forced conductors like Tubman to help guide freedom seekers all the way to Canada.

Section 03

Obstacles Faced

Massive Bounty on Her Head

By the 1850s, Maryland slaveowners offered a $40,000 reward if they could capture her.

The Fugitive Slave Act

Made it illegal to help escaped slaves anywhere in the US, so it forced traveling all the way to Canada.

Health Condition

Her constant seizures and constant sleeping spiritual experiences from her head injury made traveling dangerous and unpredictable.

Illiteracy

She could not read or write. When she heard men reading her wanted poster, she pulled out a book and pretended to read to not get caught.

Resistance from Passengers

Some people who wanted to seek freedom were frightened and wanted to turn back, which would have made the mission more horrific.

Racism

She faced discrimination as a Black woman, even among allies, when she was trying to provide safety.

Section 04

Action Plan

Specific Goal

Tubman's primary goal was to liberate as many enslaved people as possible and to accomplish the total abolition of slavery in the United States. This was for freedom, equality, and to prevent racial segregation.

Target Audience
Three Key Groups

Enslaved people in the South: They would directly benefit from Tubman's missions.

Northern abolitionists: They could provide safe housing, funding, and political force.

The Union Army: She would later join as a spy and scout during the Civil War.

Strategies
01

Underground Railway Conducting

Tubman personally led missions into the South, guiding enslaved people to the north through networks of safe houses.

02

Public Speaking

In the late 1850s, she began speaking at abolitionist meetings in the North, using her stories to build awareness and cause rallies to support her.

03

Publish Biography

She worked with Sarah Bradford to create the book Scenes in the Life of Harriet Tubman, spreading her story to audiences and gaining funding.

04

Military Awareness and Tactics

During the Civil War, she gathered the Confederates' positions, leading to impactful raids such as the Combahee River Raid.

Section 05

Take Action

Actions Taken

Before the Civil War in 1861, Tubman guided around 70 people to freedom, during a total of 13 missions into Maryland. This included her sister and her two children, her brother, and her elderly parents.

She used crucial tactics, causing her to escape on Saturday nights, using the master's horse and a wagon for the first part of the journey. Additionally, if there was any interference, she headed South to minimize conflict and spot potential slave catchers.

During the Civil War, Tubman supported the Union Army at Beaufort, South Carolina. She was working as a cook, nurse, laundress, scout, and spy. She created a network of enslaved people to gather information on Confederate camps.

In 1863, she led Colonel James Montgomery and approximately 150 Black soldiers on the Combahee River Raid. As a result, she freed over 700 enslaved people.

After the war, she made her Auburn home into the Home for Aged and Indigent Colored People. This allowed her to raise money for schools and educational opportunities for enslaved people. In 1896, she became a representative to the National Association of Colored Women's first convention allowing the right to vote.

In 1903, she donated 25 acres of land to the African Methodist Episcopal Zion Church, a shelter for poor and homeless African Americans.

Alliances Formed

William Still: Philadelphia's Underground Railroad stationmaster, who documented escapes and later turned them into a book.

Frederick Douglass: An enslaved man who became a leader, writer, and speaker against slavery in the US.

John Brown: White abolitionists who used violence, including the raid on Harpers Ferry.

Levi Coffin: Quaker abolitionist, helping thousands of enslaved people through the Underground Railroad.

In 2013, President Barack Obama assigned portions of Maryland's Eastern Shore as the "Harriet Tubman Underground Railroad National Monument," which is the first national monument honoring a Black woman.

Did You Know?

Fun Facts

Surprising and little-known details about Harriet Tubman's extraordinary life.

Never Lost a Single Passenger
In all 13 of her Underground Railroad missions, Tubman never lost a single person who was led to freedom.
She Used Medicine to Quiet Babies
To prevent crying infants from revealing the group's location, she carried a small amount of opium. This would help the babies fall asleep and not get caught by slave catchers.
She Carried a Gun on Every Mission
Tubman told anyone who wanted to turn back that she carried a pistol for defense, ensuring safety and courage for people who wanted to back down.
Always Escaped on Saturday Night
Newspapers could not be printed for those who escaped until Monday morning. This gave the group two days to escape and prevent anyone from spreading the news about the escaping people.
Paid Almost Nothing for the War
After leading the Combahee River Raid, Tubman was paid so little by the Union Army that she had to sell her own baked goods to support herself.
Nicknamed "Moses" of Her People
Led enslaved people to freedom, similar to how Moses led Israelites out of slavery in Egypt.
First Woman to Lead a US Military Operation
Tubman led the largest emancipation event of the Civil War and became the first woman to lead the military.
Contributed to the 13th Amendment
Harriet supported the cause that led to the 13th Amendment, which abolished slavery in the US.
Front of US $20
Originally chosen to appear on the front of the US $20 bill. This would make her the first African American woman, but this never happened due to security reasons.

About This Project

This website was created as a Reform Research Project exploring Harriet Tubman's role in 19th-century reform. It explores her biography, the historical context before and during the Civil War, obstacles she faced, and actions she took to create an impact on society with her extraordinary life.
